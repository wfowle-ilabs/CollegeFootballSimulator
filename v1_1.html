v1.1 — SimCast & Playstyle Identity

Watch your team play, your way

Status: Design spec for the v1.1 overhaul. Builds directly on the Architecture forward-compat hooks (PlayCaller-as-interface, watch-vs-quick-sim as a presentation toggle, coach attributes feeding play-calling). The d20 check primitive in Mechanics is unchanged — v1.1 changes what gets composed into a play and how the result is presented, not the dice.

1 What v1.1 is

v1 ships a believable, fully-simulated season you advance and read about. v1.1 makes a game something you watch and shape:

  1. SimCast — a simulation broadcast of your team’s game. Not the 3D field (that’s later); a paced, legible, ESPN-style play-by-play you watch at a chosen speed, with the real rhythm of a football game (huddles, tempo changes, timeouts, TV breaks, the two-minute drill).
  2. Gameplan — you set scheme, personnel, usage, reads, tempo, and aggression before kickoff; the AI play-caller honors it; you watch it play out. The knobs are real and they change the box score.
  3. Playstyle identity — a real playbook of formations and concepts, offensive and defensive schemes, and coach attributes wired into play-calling so all ~130 teams play distinctly. Air Raid under an aggressive coordinator should look nothing like a smashmouth option team — on both sides of the ball.
  4. Atmosphere — every program plays in a real stadium with a simulated crowd. Attendance is immersion (the broadcast shows the gate), a home-field edge in the sim (a loud house tilts the dice), and the first real driver of the future ticket → NIL economy.

The v1.1 north star: “Hit Play Game → SimCast, and watch your gameplan unfold in a real stadium against an opponent who has an identity of its own.”

This refines two v1 decisions (Decisions log): “sim & watch, no live play-calling” becomes sim & watch a gameplan you authored (still no live per-play input in SimCast), and “play catalog granularity” (open question #2) resolves toward a real playbook of formations + concepts.

2 Play Game — the game-day entry point

On your team’s game day, the matchup gets a Play Game dropdown with three options:

Option Behavior
Playcaller (grayed out in v1.1) The 3D gameday play-calling mode — you call every play from kickoff. The mode itself is later; the button and the IPlayCaller seam ship now. See Section 12.
SimCast Watch the simulation broadcast — the v1.1 build (Section 3).
Quick Sim Instant final + box score (today’s behavior; the timeline is still built so the game is reviewable play-by-play).

Non-user games are always quick-simmed (the whole week resolves instantly), exactly as v1 — SimCast/Playcaller are presentation choices over the same engine, so nothing about the sim changes based on how you watch.

Jump-In is not a Play Game option — it lives inside SimCast: while a broadcast is running you can jump into the ongoing match and take the play-calling over yourself (it hands off to Playcaller mid-game). Also grayed out in v1.1. See Section 12.

3 SimCast — the broadcast

SimCast is pure presentation. The engine produces a deterministic game; SimCast plays it back. This keeps “same seed ⇒ same game” intact while the user scrubs speed.

It’s not one scrolling log — it’s a game-presentation hub with tabs, modeled on an ESPN gamecast. A persistent header carries the scoreboard, game clock, quarter, down & distance, ball-on, possession, timeouts, the speed control (0.5× / 1× / 2× / 4× / Instant), and a (disabled) Jump-In button (Section 12).

Tab What it is
Gamecast The live view: play-by-play streaming in real time as the clock runs, with light commentary (“3rd & 4 — they’re in hurry-up…”, “BLOCKED!”). The pauses are rendered, not skipped (Section 4). This is the thing you actually watch.
Play-by-Play The structured history: each drive is an object — a collapsible card with its summary (Touchdown · 7 plays, 83 yds, 4:05) that expands to the individual plays. Mirrors the shared ESPN layout. Filter: All / Scoring / Key plays.
Box Score Per-team passing / rushing / receiving / defense / returns / kicking / punting — the existing BoxScorePage, now updating live.
Team Stats The team-comparison grid: 1st downs, 3rd/4th-down efficiency, total yards, yards/play, penalties, turnovers, time of possession.
Game Info Stadium, location, attendance, weather, network (Section 8).

Speed only changes the playback cadence; it never touches the RNG or the outcome. Quick Sim lands you straight on the final Box Score, but the timeline is still built — so a quick-simmed game is reviewable play-by-play (the architecture’s spectator-mode hook).

3.1 The structured game timeline

The foundation for all of the above (and the thing the current engine lacks). Today the sim emits a List<string> play log; v1.1 emits a typed, drive-grouped timeline every tab reads from.

GameTimeline
└─ Drive[]                 ← a possession: team, start/end field pos, result, plays, yards, time of poss
   └─ Segment[]            ← ordered; each carries a clock stamp + a real-time "broadcast duration"
      ├─ Snap              ← a play: call (formation+concept), participants, the check chain, the result
      ├─ Pause             ← huddle / sub / timeout / TV timeout / 2-min / injury / measurement
      ├─ Score             ← TD / FG / XP / 2-pt / safety / return TD (the beats to emphasize)
      └─ PossessionChange  ← punt / turnover / downs / kickoff (with returns)
  • Drives are first-class — that’s what the Play-by-Play tab renders as cards, and what a drive chart summarizes. (Today drives are simulated but not retained as objects.)
  • The timeline is computed by the sim, deterministically. The string play log becomes a derived view of it, so logs, box scores, drive cards, and the Gamecast all read one source.
  • Broadcast duration per segment is a presentation hint (a sack lingers, a hurry-up snap is quick); the chosen speed multiplies it. It is not the football clock — that’s the game-clock runoff on each Snap/Pause (Section 4).

4 Clock & pacing fidelity

The “pauses in the record” require a real game-flow model. Today GameClock is an elapsed-seconds counter with fixed per-play runoff and no stoppages (deferred at M2). v1.1 makes it faithful to college rules — which differ from the NFL in two ways that matter:

College clock rules we model (current NCAA):

  • First down, in bounds → clock keeps running. Since the 2023 rule change, gaining a first down no longer stops the clock — except inside the last two minutes of a half, where a first down stops it. (This is the big NFL difference and it speeds college games up.)
  • Two-minute timeout (new in 2024). The clock stops at 2:00 in the 2nd and 4th quarters — a built-in stoppage, not a TV timeout.
  • Incomplete pass → clock stops until the next snap.
  • Out of bounds → clock stops, then restarts on the ready-for-play signal — except inside the last two minutes of a half, where it stays stopped until the snap.
  • Change of possession (punt / turnover / downs) and penalty → clock stops, restarts on the ready signal.
  • Score → clock stops for the try (XP/2-pt) and the kickoff.
  • Play clock 40s (25s after administrative stoppages); the pre-snap burn is where tempo lives.

On top of the stop rules:

Element Model
Per-play runoff Pre-snap burn (tempo) + the play’s duration. Huddle = normal; no-huddle/hurry-up = minimal pre-snap burn; bleed-clock = milk the play clock to ~:01.
Timeouts 3 per half per team; spent by game-management logic (stop the clock trailing late, ice a kicker, avoid delay-of-game), driven by coach GameManagement.
TV timeouts Inserted at quarter breaks and after some changes of possession — the broadcast rhythm (separate from the 2-minute stop).
Two-minute drill End-of-half behavior: hurry-up, get-out-of-bounds, spikes, clock awareness — gated by score + time + coach.
Tempo as identity A team’s default tempo is part of its scheme/gameplan (Air Raid pushes pace; smashmouth bleeds it), so pacing itself differentiates teams.

Each stoppage becomes a Pause (or a runoff annotation on a Snap) in the timeline, so the Gamecast renders the rhythm and the football clock stays honest. Clock realism is what makes a comeback drive feel real — the difference between college and NFL clock math is the difference between a team having time and not.

5 Gameplan — the deterministic control surface

The knobs. You set these pre-game; the AI caller honors them; they are part of the deterministic inputs (same gameplan + seed ⇒ same game). For non-user teams, an AI sets a gameplan from the team’s scheme, coach, and roster.

Knob What it controls
Scheme The team’s playbook + identity on each side of the ball (Section 6).
Personnel / on-field Which players are in for a given personnel grouping (pulls from the depth chart per formation).
Carry share How carries split across the RB room (feature back vs. committee).
Target share How looks split across receivers (a true #1 vs. spread-it-around).
Read order The 1st / 2nd / 3rd reads in the pass game (target priority within a concept).
QB archetype Pocket passer (dropback, climbs the pocket) vs. dual-threat (designed QB runs, scramble, RPO pull) — changes the run/pass surface and the scramble branch.
Tempo Default pace + situational (hurry-up when trailing, bleed when leading).
Aggression 4th-down go-for-it tendency, deep-shot rate, blitz rate (defense) — a baseline the coach modulates.

The depth chart (today just “starter ordering”) grows a thin usage layer: per-position role slots + the shares above. Promotion still works; usage is the new dimension.

6 Schemes & the playbook

v1.1 commits to a real playbook of formations and concepts (open question #2, resolved). The d20 chain is unchanged; concepts decide which checks compose and with what modifiers.

6.1 The three tiers

Formation   ← personnel + alignment  (who's on the field, where)
   └─ Concept   ← the play             (what they run)
        └─ Check chain  ← from Mechanics — the d20 resolution, with concept-specific modifiers

Offensive formations (sample): Shotgun Spread (11), Singleback, I-Form, Pistol, Empty, Goal-line (heavy). Offensive concepts (sample):

Family Concepts
Run Inside Zone, Outside Zone, Power, Counter, Draw, QB Read/Option, Jet Sweep
Pass Four Verts, Mesh, Smash, Flood, Slant-Flat, Screen (RB/bubble), Play-Action Shot
RPO Zone-read + bubble / slant / glance (a single snap with a read that resolves run or quick throw)

Defensive fronts (sample): 4-3, 3-4, Nickel, Dime, 46/Bear, Goal-line. Defensive concepts (sample): Cover 1 (man-free), Cover 2, Cover 3, Cover 4 (quarters), Cover 0 blitz, zone blitz, stunt/base. A call = front + coverage + pressure.

6.2 A scheme is a weighted playbook

A Scheme is a distribution over formations + concepts plus identity modifiers:

  • Air Raid → heavy Shotgun/Empty; pass-weighted (Mesh, Verts, RPO); fast tempo; deep-shot lean.
  • Spread-RPO → Shotgun/Pistol; balanced run/pass via RPO; up-tempo.
  • Pro-Style → multiple personnel; balanced, play-action-heavy; pocket QB lean.
  • Smashmouth / Power → I-Form/Singleback; Power/Counter/PA; slow tempo.
  • Option → Pistol/Flexbone; zone-read/option/QB run; dual-threat QB.

Defensive schemes mirror this: 4-3 base, 3-4 multiple, nickel/dime pass-D, man-press aggressive, bend-don’t-break zone — each a distribution over fronts/coverages/pressures with a blitz-rate identity.

6.3 Read progression & QB archetype

A pass concept carries an ordered list of route options mapped to receiver roles (X / Z / slot / TE / RB) — the read order from the gameplan. On the snap:

  1. The protection check sets a pressure clock (OL vs. rush).
  2. The QB works the progression: read 1 vs. its defender → if covered, read 2 → read 3, each gated by QB ReadProgression + Awareness and shrinking pocket time.
  3. Dual-threat QBs branch to scramble when the pocket breaks or reads are covered (and own the designed-run / RPO-pull concepts); pocket passers climb and check it down.

This is exactly the “1st / 2nd / 3rd reads, dual-threat vs pocket” surface — emergent from the concept × archetype × coverage matchup.

7 Coaching styles

The Coach attribute sheet already exists (Architecture) and is unused by the sim today. v1.1 wires it in:

Attribute Effect
PlayCalling (aggression/tendency) Shifts the scheme distribution toward aggressive concepts; raises 4th-down go / deep-shot / blitz rates.
OffensiveAcumen / DefensiveAcumen Grant advantage on the matchup when the call out-schemes the opponent; sharpen situational selection.
GameManagement Drives clock/timeout decisions (Section 4) — when to burn a timeout, ice a kicker, go hurry-up.
Motivation Composure/advantage buffs in big spots (already the architecture’s intent).

A team’s identity is the product: Scheme × Coach × Personnel × Gameplan. That product is what makes 130 teams distinct rather than 130 copies with different rosters.

8 Stadiums & atmosphere

Every team gets a home stadium. v1.1 scope is location + capacity (the environment) — not the deferred economy plumbing.

  • Stadium — name, city, state, capacity (room later for surface, elevation, year built). It hangs off the program’s venue identity (and moves to the University / Athletics Department once the economy arrives — referenced by ID either way).
  • Data fill — assign each of the ~130 FBS programs its real home stadium (name / city / capacity), a one-time addition to FbsLeagueData alongside the existing team/conference/prestige data.

The SimCast Game Info panel surfaces stadium, city, attendance, and network — the ESPN “Game Information” card.

9 Attendance simulation

Attendance isn’t flavor — it’s a sim input and the first real driver of the future economy.

What drives the gate (a deterministic function, like everything else in the sim):

  • Stadium capacity — the ceiling.
  • Home prestige + record — winners draw.
  • Opponent quality / ranking and rivalry — a ranked rival packs the house; a cupcake doesn’t.
  • Time of season + day/time slot — a November primetime conference game ≠ a Week 1 noon tune-up.
  • (Later: weather, TV window.)

→ yields an attendance number and a % of capacity — “how loud is it.”

Effect on the sim — home-field advantage. The engine has no HFA today; this adds it. Crowd strength (% of capacity) tilts the dice through the existing advantage system (Mechanics) — the architecture’s “home crowd grants advantage,” finally real:

  • The road offense feels it most: pre-snap communication / false-start risk and Composure checks on third down, in the red zone, and in the fourth quarter of a close game.
  • It scales with % of capacity (a half-empty stadium is no edge; a sold-out night game is) and is dampened by the road team’s Composure.
  • It is a deterministic input: same matchup + seed ⇒ same crowd ⇒ same edge.

The economic hook (seam now, flow later). Attendance → ticket revenue → Athletics Department budget → (v2) NIL / facilities / staff. v1.1 introduces attendance + HFA; the money flow stays in the v2 economy, but this is the input it consumes. Empty stadium → weak gate → no NIL → hard to stay competitive: the loop starts here.

10 The IPlayCaller seam

Today PlayCaller is a static class with one passBias knob. v1.1 makes it the documented interface:

  • AiPlayCaller composes Scheme × Coach × Gameplan × Situation → a concrete call. Both the user team (honoring the user’s gameplan) and every AI team use it.
  • UserPlayCaller is the seam left for Jump-In (Section 12) — a future implementation that takes calls from a human mid-game. Not built in v1.1; the interface and the disabled button are.
  • PlayResolver gains concept dispatch: each concept declares which checks it runs and its modifiers, composed from the same CheckResolver primitive.

11 Determinism boundary

Unchanged in principle, explicit for the new pieces:

  • Gameplan + scheme + coach are deterministic inputs. Same save + seed + gameplans ⇒ identical game. The gameplan joins the seed as part of the reproducible state.
  • Attendance & home-field advantage are deterministic inputs too. The gate is a function of the matchup; the crowd edge it produces is computed before kickoff and feeds the dice the same way every replay. Crowd affects the sim; it is not presentation.
  • SimCast speed and all broadcast pacing are pure presentation. They read the already-computed timeline; they never call the RNG. Scrubbing speed, pausing, or replaying cannot change a single yard.
  • The timeline is an artifact, like media articles — derived from the deterministic game, never an input to it.

12 Forward hook — Playcaller & Jump-In

The eventual 3D gameday play-calling mode (where you stop watching and call the plays) has two entry points, and v1.1 lays the seam for both so the later mode bolts on without a rewrite:

  • Playcaller — from the Play Game dropdown: enter the mode at kickoff and call every play. (Grayed out in v1.1.)
  • Jump-In — a control inside SimCast: while the broadcast runs, jump into the ongoing match and take the calls over from there. (Grayed out in v1.1.)

Both resolve to the same thing under the hood:

  • The IPlayCaller interface — a UserPlayCaller drops in for the user team in place of AiPlayCaller (Section 10).
  • The framing that SimCast and live-calling are two presentations over one engine — watching vs. driving — so “jump in” mid-game just swaps the user team’s caller, it doesn’t change the sim.

The 3D rendering and the live play-calling UX themselves remain out of scope (later than v1.1); v1.1 ships the interface and the two disabled buttons.

13 Phasing

Each phase is independently demoable.

13.1 P1 — SimCast + pacing + atmosphere

  • The GameTimeline (drives → segments) + the string log as a derived view.
  • The college clock/pacing model: tempo runoff, the college stop rules, timeouts (coach-driven), TV timeouts, two-minute timeout.
  • Stadiums (data fill) + attendance + home-field advantage wired into the dice.
  • The SimCast hub: header (scoreboard/clock/speed/disabled Jump-In) + tabs Gamecast / Play-by-Play (drive cards) / Box Score / Team Stats / Game Info; the Play Game dropdown (Playcaller-disabled / SimCast / Quick Sim).
  • Demo: Play Game → SimCast, watch a real, paced game in a real stadium at 1× / 2× / instant, then drill the Play-by-Play drives.

13.2 P2 — Gameplan + usage knobs

  • The Gameplan model + the depth-chart usage layer (carry/target share, read order, personnel).
  • The IPlayCaller refactor (AiPlayCaller); resolver usage-weighting; QB archetype + read progression.
  • Gameplan editor UI for the user team.
  • Demo: author a gameplan (feature back, true #1 receiver, dual-threat QB) and watch the box score reflect it in SimCast.

13.3 P3 — Schemes & coaching styles

  • The playbook (formations + concepts) + concept dispatch in the resolver.
  • Offensive & defensive schemes as weighted playbooks; coach attributes wired into calling, clock, and advantage.
  • AI sets a per-team gameplan from scheme + coach + roster → league-wide distinct identities.
  • Demo: scout any team and see a real scheme; watch two contrasting styles (Air Raid vs. smashmouth) collide.

13.4 P4 — Polish & balance

  • Drive chart, SimCast narrative beats, richer stat overlays.
  • Balance pass so the new playstyles produce believable stat lines (scoring, run/pass splits, completion %, explosive-play rates) — the M9-style tuning, now with identities to tune against.
  • Demo: a multi-season run whose team-by-team stat profiles read like real, distinct programs.

14 Open questions for v1.1

  1. Playbook breadth — how many formations/concepts per scheme is enough for distinct identities without ballooning the caller/resolver? (Start lean, grow.)
  2. Concept × coverage matchup table — the modifier matrix (e.g., Cover 0 vs. a deep shot = boom/bust; zone vs. mesh = soft) that makes scheme-vs-scheme legible.
  3. Pacing realism vs. watchability — how much dead time to render before it drags, even at 1×; default speed and skip rules.
  4. AI gameplan generation — how a team’s scheme + coach + roster yields a believable weekly gameplan (and how much it adapts week to week).
  5. Timeline cost at scale — building the full timeline for ~65 quick-simmed games/week must stay near-instant (it’s cheap, but confirm).
  6. Save growth — is a reviewable timeline persisted for the user’s games, or rebuilt on demand? (Likely rebuilt; only final/box persists.)
  7. Jump-In handoff semantics — when the future live mode swaps the caller mid-game, how state/među determinism is framed (a documented seam now, resolved when that mode is built).
  8. Home-field-advantage strength — how much edge a packed house should be; a tuning surface (too much and home teams never lose). Crowd grants advantage vs. a flat modifier — which, where?
  9. Stadium data — sourcing the ~130 stadium names / cities / capacities, and handling realignment / relocations over many seasons.
  10. Commentary depth — templated play descriptions vs. richer color for the Gamecast; can it reuse the Media INarrativeGenerator seam (templated now, LLM later)?
