Architecture

College Football Simulator — v1

Status: Living design document. Engine: Godot 4.6.3 · Language: C# / .NET · Genre: Single-player season-management simulation.

1 Design Philosophy

Three principles drive every decision below.

  1. Simulation over presentation (for v1). The engine is the product. The UI exists to start a save, configure a team, and watch outcomes. We build a correct, observable simulation core first; polish comes later.
  2. No “overall” rating — character sheets, not star ratings. Players, coaches, and staff are described by attributes and skills the way a Baldur’s Gate III character is. A player is not “an 87 OVR”; he is a bundle of numbers (Strength 14, Awareness 11, Route Running 16…) that produce emergent results. This makes development, scheme fit, and matchups fall out of the math rather than being hand-tuned.
  3. Outcomes are dice, not formulas with noise bolted on. Every contested event resolves as a d20 check against a Difficulty Class (DC) modified by attributes. The randomness is the design, not a fudge factor. This keeps results legible (“he failed the block check, DL won the rep”) and tunable.

2 What We Simulate vs. What We Don’t

This boundary is the most important thing in this document. Everything in “Out” is deliberately deferred so v1 can ship.

3 World Hierarchy & Ownership Model

College football is not just teams and games — it’s an institutional pyramid where money and membership flow downward and results flow upward. Modeling this hierarchy correctly in v1 (even where most levels are thin) is what makes the v1→v2 leap possible: v2’s economy (TV deals → budgets → NIL, recruiting, facilities) bolts directly onto this skeleton instead of forcing a restructure.

3.1 The ownership pyramid

Division                     ── e.g. FBS (FBS-IAA). The competitive tier.
└─ Conference                ── membership group; negotiates media rights.
   ├─ TV / Media Deal        ── owned by the conference; distributes revenue to members.
   └─ University   (member)  ── the institution.
      └─ Athletics Department── holds the budget; runs all sports.
         └─ Team  (football) ── the football program — what we play & sim.
            ├─ Roster (Players)
            ├─ Coaches & Staff
            └─ Depth chart / scheme

Two distinct relationships live here, and keeping them separate is what makes realignment and economics clean later:

  • Containment / ownership (downward): Division → Conference → University → Athletics Department → Team → Roster. The literal tree of who-owns-what.
  • Membership (sideways): a University (via its Team) belongs to a Conference for competition and revenue-sharing. Conference realignment = re-pointing this membership edge without touching the ownership chain.

The TV/Media Deal hangs off the Conference, not the Team — deliberate and true to life: conferences sell media rights collectively and distribute to members through their athletics departments.

3.2 What each level holds — v1 vs. later

Level v1 (modeled now) Later (v2+)
Division (FBS-IAA) Identity, its set of conferences, postseason/CFP rules. Other divisions (FCS→JUCO); multi-division play.
Conference Members, divisions/pods, scheduling rules, tiebreakers, championship-game config, auto-bid logic. Realignment, expansion, TV deal negotiation.
TV / Media Deal Stub attached to each conference (nominal value field, unused by sim). Real contracts: value, term, distribution formula → feeds AD budgets.
University Identity (name, location, brand, prestige). Enrollment, academics affecting recruiting, brand growth.
Athletics Department Thin: owns the football Team, carries a budget field (unused by v1 sim). Budget from media/tickets/donors → allocates to NIL pool, recruiting spend, facility/stadium projects, coaching salaries.
Team (football) Fully modeled: roster, staff, depth chart, scheme, record — the unit we simulate. NIL spending, facility ratings affecting development, recruiting pitch.

Principle: v1 builds the whole tree but only puts behavior on Team (and the game engine). Division/Conference get the structural logic they need for scheduling & postseason; University/AthleticsDepartment/TVDeal exist as data shells with the fields v2 will animate. Nothing in v1 has to be torn down to add the economy.

3.3 The future economic flow (v2+, sketched now so v1 doesn’t block it)

        ▼

This is not in v1, but every box maps to an entity in Section 3.1 — so v2 is populating fields and adding services, not restructuring the world.

4 The Resolution System (d20 + DC)

The beating heart of the engine. A single, reusable check resolves every contested football event.

4.1 The check

roll  = d20  (1–20)
total = roll + attributeModifier + situationalModifiers
result:
    roll == 20            → CRITICAL SUCCESS   (best-case outcome, ignores DC)
    roll == 1             → CRITICAL FAILURE   (blunder: fumble, sack, blown coverage)
    total >= DC           → SUCCESS
    total <  DC           → FAILURE
margin = total - DC       (used to scale magnitude, e.g. yards gained)
  • Attribute modifier: derived from the relevant attribute(s) (see Section 8). BG3-style: mod = floor((attr - 10) / 2) is our starting curve, but we keep this in one tunable function so we can reshape it.
  • DC: set by the opposing unit/player’s relevant attributes plus situation. A run block is OL.RunBlock checked against a DC derived from DL.BlockShed.
  • Advantage / Disadvantage: roll 2d20, take higher (advantage) or lower (disadvantage). Granted by scheme fit, coaching edges, momentum, home crowd, fatigue mismatches. This is how we express “edges” without inflating raw numbers.
  • Margin scales magnitude. Beating a DC by 1 ≠ beating it by 12. A run play’s yardage, a pass’s air yards, all scale off margin through a transfer curve.

4.2 Determinism & reproducibility

All randomness flows through a single seeded RandomNumberGenerator owned by the simulation context. Same save + same seed + same inputs ⇒ identical game. Essential for debugging, save integrity, and “replay this game” later. We never call GD.Randi() / global RNG inside sim logic.

4.3 Why this and not probability tables

A play-by-play d20 engine gives us legible, debuggable outcomes (“OL_LeftGuard failed RunBlock check vs DT, DC 16, rolled 7+4=11 → penetration, RB tackled for -2”) and a uniform mechanic across blocking, coverage, tackling, kicking, and decision-making. Tuning happens in modifier curves and DC tables, isolated from the dice.

5 Simulation Layers (top-down)

The sim is a nest of loops, each layer producing events the layer below resolves. Each layer is its own service so it can be tested in isolation.

SeasonSimulator
  └─ for each week:
       WeekSimulator
         └─ for each scheduled game:
              GameSimulator                      ← clock, score, possession, weather
                └─ DriveSimulator                ← field position, drive state
                     └─ PlaySimulator            ← chooses play, runs the play
                          ├─ PlayCaller          ← offense/defense pick plays (coach attrs + situation)
                          └─ PlayResolver        ← d20 checks → yards / TO / score / penalty
                               └─ CheckResolver  ← the d20-vs-DC primitive
  └─ after regular season: ConferenceChampionships → CFPBracket → bowls → Champion
  └─ after each week: update stats, standings, rankings, injuries, fatigue recovery
  └─ after the season: OffseasonStage
        ├─ archive completed season → historical state
        ├─ advance class years · graduate & depart seniors
        ├─ PlayerGenerator → fill open slots with walk-ons (v1 recruiting placeholder)
        └─ build next Season → loop   (v1 runs season after season)

Key idea: PlayResolver composes many CheckResolver calls. A single pass play might resolve: protection (OL vs DL checks) → QB decision (Awareness check) → throw (Accuracy vs DC from distance/coverage) → catch (Catching vs DC from coverage/contact) → YAC (Agility vs Tackling). Each is one d20 check; the play outcome is their composition.

6 Event-Driven Architecture & Side Effects

Game modules don’t reach into each other’s side effects. The simulation emits typed domain events, and independent subscribers react. This keeps the sim core ignorant of presentation, media, stats, and everything else — they’re all just listeners.

  • Producers: the simulators emit domain events as things happen — GameConcluded, WeekAdvanced, UpsetOccurred, RankingsUpdated, PlayerInjured, RecordBroken, SeasonConcluded.
  • Bus: EventBus (autoload) dispatches events to subscribers (typed C# events / Godot signals).
  • Consumers (side effects): the Media & Narrative subsystem (Section 7) is the flagship consumer; stats aggregation, standings/rankings refresh, injury bookkeeping, and — later — achievements, awards watches, and recruiting buzz all hang off the same events.

Two rules keep this clean:

  1. Events are facts, not commands. A producer announces what happened; it never knows who listens. Adding a new consumer means subscribing, not editing the sim.
  2. Consumers never mutate state the sim depends on. Side effects are strictly downstream. The deterministic core (sim + seeded RNG) is the single source of truth; consumers read events and produce their own artifacts (articles, stat tables, UI updates). This is exactly what lets non-deterministic work like LLM text generation live in the system without breaking “same seed ⇒ same season.”

Consumers may also process events asynchronously on worker threads (e.g. narrative generation between weeks), so advancing the season never blocks on a side effect.

SeasonSimulator / GameSimulator ──emit──▶ EventBus
                                            │  (GameConcluded, WeekAdvanced, UpsetOccurred …)
                ┌──────────────┬────────────┴───────────┬──────────────────┐
                ▼              ▼                         ▼                  ▼
         StatAggregator   RankingService          MediaSubsystem      (future: awards,
                                                  (async / threaded)   achievements, buzz)

7 Media & Narrative Subsystem

An in-game ESPN-style site for immersion: weekly recaps, season storylines, a Featured tab, and the ability to look up your team or any other team and read its coverage. It is pure presentation — a consumer of Section 6, derived from deterministic GameResult data.

Pipeline (between weeks, in the background):

  1. WeekAdvanced / GameConcluded events land on the EventBus.
  2. MediaSubsystem builds a NarrativeContext per story — the facts a writer needs: final score, key plays, standout stat lines, standings/ranking implications, streaks, rivalry/upset flags.
  3. A tiering pass decides coverage and article type (game recap, upset alert, ranking shake-up, season storyline).
  4. The chosen INarrativeGenerator turns each NarrativeContext into a NewsArticle.
  5. Articles are written to the sidecar media cache and surfaced in the Media menu.

Steps 2–5 run off the main thread, kicked off when a week concludes, so the player can keep advancing; articles stream into the Media menu as they finish.

Coverage tiering (quality-per-compute; scales to ~65 games/week): featured / Top-25 / rivalry / upset / user-team games get full articles; everything else gets a short templated recap + box score. The Featured tab surfaces the week’s biggest stories.

Pluggable generation — INarrativeGenerator:

  • TemplateNarrativeGenerator — v1 baseline. Deterministic, instant, no model dependency; fills authored templates from the NarrativeContext. Always available, and the fallback for low-tier games.
  • LlmNarrativeGenerator — drop-in upgrade. A small local model (~7B) via LLamaSharp loading a bundled GGUF, fully on-device (no 3rd-party API, no per-call cost). It’s handed a constrained “script”: a structured prompt that injects the NarrativeContext facts and dictates format/length/tone, so a small model stays factual and on-brand. Slots in behind the same interface with zero changes elsewhere.

This mirrors the PlayCaller-as-interface pattern (Section 12): the consumer is stable; the implementation swaps.

Determinism boundary. Articles are artifacts, never inputs — regenerating one can’t change a score or a standing. Persisting them (below) also means a reloaded save shows the same text the player first read.

Storage — sidecar media cache. Articles persist in a per-save media store separate from the core SaveGame (e.g. user://saves/<slot>.media), keyed by week + team + article id. It survives reload, lets the user re-read past coverage, and keeps the core save lean.

8 Attribute & Skill Model (BG3-inspired)

No overall rating. A Player carries core attributes (broad, slow-changing, BG3’s six-stat analog) and position skills (specific, what actually gets checked on a play).

8.1 Core attributes (every player has these)

The working set (numbers, scale, and the blend math live in Mechanics):

  • Strength — power blocking, breaking/making tackles, press.
  • Agility — change of direction, route breaks, shedding.
  • Speed — straight-line; gates breakaways and pursuit.
  • Awareness — football IQ; reads, audibles, coverage recognition, penalty avoidance.
  • Durability — injury-resistance & fatigue curve (not a per-play check; modifies injury/fatigue rolls).
  • Composure — performance under pressure (late game, hostile crowd, big stage); affects advantage/disadvantage.

8.2 Position skills (subset relevant per position)

Examples (full matrix → attributes reference):

  • QB: ThrowPower, ShortAccuracy, DeepAccuracy, PlayAction, Scramble, ReadProgression.
  • RB: Vision, Elusiveness, Trucking, BallSecurity, PassBlock, Receiving.
  • WR/TE: RouteRunning, Catching, ContestedCatch, Release, RunBlock (TE).
  • OL: RunBlock, PassBlock, PullSpeed, AnchorStrength.
  • DL/EDGE: BlockShed, PassRush, RunStop, Pursuit.
  • LB: Coverage, RunStop, Blitz, Tackling.
  • DB: ManCoverage, ZoneCoverage, BallSkills, PressTechnique, Tackling.
  • ST: KickPower, KickAccuracy, PuntPower, Returns.

A check looks up the relevant skill (or a blend of skill + core attribute) for both participants and resolves through the system in Section 4.

8.3 Coaches & staff

Coaches are character sheets too — no “overall.” Attributes such as: OffensiveAcumen, DefensiveAcumen, PlayCalling (aggression/tendency), Development (per-position growth bias), Discipline (penalty rate), GameManagement (clock/timeouts/4th-down), Recruiting (stubbed for v1), Motivation (Composure/advantage buffs). Coach attributes feed the PlayCaller (what plays get chosen) and grant advantage/disadvantage at the check layer, rather than directly editing player numbers mid-game.

8.4 Derived vs. stored

We store attributes. We derive any “rating-like” number on demand (e.g. a scouting view) from attributes through a position-weighted function — never the reverse. This keeps the BG3 ethos intact and means a “rating” is just one lens on the sheet.

8.5 Player generation (initial rosters & walk-ons)

Rosters aren’t hand-authored — a deterministic PlayerGenerator builds them, drawing on the seeded RNG (Section 4) so a given save reproduces the same players. It serves two jobs:

  1. Initial league fill at new-save: ~130 FBS teams’ worth of players with position-appropriate attribute/skill sheets, class years, names, and physicals.
  2. Walk-on replenishment each offseason: the v1 stand-in for recruiting — open scholarship/depth slots are filled with generated walk-ons so rosters stay whole season to season.

Generation is prestige-weighted: higher-prestige programs draw from stronger distributions, a rough proxy for recruiting pull until real recruiting arrives. The generator is also the seam recruiting plugs into later — a RecruitingService replaces the walk-on step (job 2) without touching initial generation (job 1, which still seeds new leagues and, eventually, lower divisions).

9 Data Model — Core Classes

In Godot+C#, the persistent data model is built from custom Resource types ([GlobalClass] public partial class X : Resource). Resources serialize cleanly via ResourceSaver/ResourceLoader, show in the editor inspector, and are the idiomatic Godot way to hold game data. Pure-logic services (simulators) are plain C# classes, not nodes — they take data in and return results, keeping them unit-testable without the scene tree.

Resource (data — serialized)
├─ Player              : attributes, skills, position, class/year, depth slot, fatigue,
│                         injury status; current-season stats live in the season sidecar,
│                         career stat lines archive to the historical sidecar (by player ID)
├─ Coach               : role (HC/OC/DC), attributes, tendencies
├─ StaffMember         : trainers/specialists as attribute bundles (lightweight in v1)
├─ Team                : identity (name, abbr, colors, venue, prestige),
│                         roster (Player[]), staff, depth chart, scheme/gameplan, record
├─ AthleticsDepartment : owns the Team; budget field (thin in v1 — see World Hierarchy)
├─ University          : institution identity/brand; owns its AthleticsDepartment
├─ TVDeal              : media-rights stub attached to a Conference (thin in v1)
├─ Conference          : member universities/teams, divisions/pods, tiebreak rules,
│                         champ-game config, TVDeal
├─ Division            : competitive tier (FBS for v1); member conferences, postseason/CFP rules
├─ League              : root world container (holds the FBS Division in v1)
├─ Schedule            : weeks → list of Matchup (home, away, site, week, type)
├─ GameResult          : final score, box score, play log (empty when quick-simmed), key events
├─ Season              : year, current week, Schedule, standings, rankings, postseason state
└─ SaveGame (spine)    : entity registry (ID→sidecar) · user team id · current-season
                          pointer · RNG seed/state · schema version · sidecar manifest
                          (bulk data lives in sidecars it references by ID — see @sec-storage)

Plain C# (logic — not serialized)
├─ SeasonSimulator / WeekSimulator / GameSimulator / DriveSimulator / PlaySimulator
├─ PlayCaller (offense + defense)
├─ PlayResolver
├─ CheckResolver           ← the d20-vs-DC primitive
├─ RankingService, StandingsService, TiebreakService
├─ CfpBracketBuilder, ScheduleBuilder
├─ StatAggregator
├─ PlayerGenerator             ← deterministic roster & walk-on generation (@sec-player-gen)
├─ OffseasonStage              ← archive → age roster → walk-on replenish → next season
├─ SaveManager + ISaveModule   ← spine + per-module sidecar load/save (@sec-storage)
├─ EventBus + typed domain events (GameConcluded, WeekAdvanced, UpsetOccurred, …)
└─ MediaSubsystem + INarrativeGenerator (TemplateNarrativeGenerator | LlmNarrativeGenerator)

C# serialization note: Fields meant to persist must be Godot-visible — use [Export] properties and Godot collection types (Godot.Collections.Array<T>, Godot.Collections.Dictionary) on Resource subclasses, since plain List<T>/.NET dictionaries don’t serialize through ResourceSaver. Confirmed against Godot 4.6 docs.

Media types (see Section 7): NewsArticle : Resource (headline, body, type, week, team refs, timestamp) lives in the per-save sidecar media cache, not the core SaveGame graph. The EventBus, domain events, MediaSubsystem, and INarrativeGenerator (TemplateNarrativeGenerator / LlmNarrativeGenerator) are plain C#.

10 Storage & Save Architecture

Over a long, multi-season run the interesting data — detailed stats, player career logs, season history — only grows. To keep the hot path small and let that complexity expand gracefully, a save is not one monolithic file. It’s a thin spine plus a set of per-module sidecars, generalizing the media-cache pattern.

10.1 The spine (main save file)

Small, always loaded first. Holds only what’s needed to bootstrap and to resolve everything else:

  • Entity registry — ID → name → which sidecar for every entity. The one piece of glue that lets sidecars reference each other without knowing about each other’s files.
  • Pointers & identity — user team, current-season pointer, settings.
  • RNG seed + state — so a resumed save continues deterministically (Section 4).
  • Schema version + sidecar manifest — which files belong to this save, and at what version.

The original plan was ResourceSaver/ResourceLoader (.tres/.res).

As built (M5): to keep CfbSim.Core engine-free and headlessly testable, persistence is implemented as portable JSON (System.Text.Json) inside the core — one file per sidecar (spine.json, league.json, season.json, history.json, archive.json, media.json), written atomically (temp + rename) and version-stamped via the spine. The Godot side just hands the core a globalized user://saves/<slot> path. Same spine-plus-sidecars shape, just a different (Godot-independent) serializer. Box scores are an exception — kept in a per-session memory cache, not yet persisted.

10.2 Module sidecars (v1 partition)

Sidecar Holds Loaded when
current-state The world as it is now: teams, rosters, players’ current attributes, staff, depth charts, schemes, prestige. At load — it’s the live league.
season-state The in-flight season: schedule, results, standings, rankings, week pointer, postseason bracket, current-season stats. While playing a season.
historical-state Completed seasons: final standings, champions, year-by-year team records, archived player career stat lines. When browsing history / records.
media NewsArticles, keyed by week + team (Section 7). When the Media menu opens.
stats-index Cross-cutting rollups: league leaders, all-time records. For leaderboards; updated incrementally.

Don’t over-shard. This five-way split is the whole v1 partition — not a sidecar per entity. Granularity is a knob we turn later (see Growing complexity, below).

10.3 The one rule that makes it work: reference by ID, never by pointer

The trap isn’t “this data references other data” — almost everything does. The trap is two files that must be loaded together. We avoid it with a single discipline:

  • Cross-module links are stored as IDs (StringName/int), never as live Resource references.
  • IDs are resolved through the spine’s registry, which says which sidecar holds the target and loads it on demand. A sidecar references IDs, not other sidecars — they never point at each other directly, and none needs a sibling eagerly loaded to deserialize.

Godot specifics: if a Resource @exports another Resource, loading the parent eagerly pulls the child — exactly the coupling we don’t want across modules. So cross-module fields are plain ID values, resolved with ResourceLoader.Load on demand. Within a module, real sub-resource references are fine.

10.4 The cross-cutting exception

Some data is owned by one module but queried across all — national stat leaders, all-time records. Rebuilding those by loading every team’s sidecar would defeat the purpose, so they get a dedicated stats-index, maintained incrementally as domain events fire (Section 6). It’s the one deliberate central rollup.

10.5 Growing complexity over time

When a module’s dataset becomes genuinely heavy — say, full play-by-play career logs per player — it’s promoted to its own sidecar, referenced by ID like everything else. No other module changes; the registry just learns a new mapping. That’s the whole point: we can deepen stat-keeping and history without a migration that touches the rest of the game.

10.6 Integrity

  • Atomic writes: each sidecar is written to a temp file and renamed into place, so a crash mid-save can’t leave a half-written file.
  • Version-stamping: every sidecar carries the spine’s save/schema version; a mismatch on load is detectable (and migratable).
  • One abstraction, paid once: every module implements the same ISaveModule (Save / Load / version), so the machinery lives in one SaveManager and each module stays trivial — keeping this from becoming “significant complexity.”

This refines the earlier single-SaveGame-graph idea: the SaveGame resource is now the spine, and the bulk lives in sidecars it references by ID. (We still avoid the node-group save()/JSON-line pattern from the Godot docs — that suits scene-tree-resident state; our model is data Resources, which ResourceSaver handles directly.)

Core/engine split (refinement, in place since M1): the simulation lives in a CfbSim.Core class library with zero Godot dependency, so it’s unit-testable headlessly with dotnet test (no scene tree, no engine). The Godot project references it. Consequently the domain model is plain C# for now; Godot Resource types and serialization (Section 10) are introduced at the persistence boundary (M5), either by adding the Resource base to the model or via thin adapter Resources. Randomness uses our own seeded PCG32 (IRng) rather than Godot’s RNG, which keeps the core engine-free and gives stronger cross-platform reproducibility.

Autoload singletons (Godot autoloads):

  • GameManager — owns the active SaveGame, current screen flow, new-game/continue.
  • SimDriver — bridges UI requests (“sim this week”, “sim to end of season”) to the plain-C# simulators and surfaces results/events.
  • EventBus — the domain-event hub (Section 6). Simulators emit typed events; UI, stats, rankings, and the Media subsystem all subscribe — the sim never knows who listens.
  • MediaSubsystem — listens for game/week events and generates narratives on a background thread into the sidecar media cache, feeding the Media menu (Section 7).

The simulators themselves stay out of the scene tree — autoloads are thin coordinators that own data and call into pure logic.

12 Forward-Compatibility Hooks (so v2+ doesn’t require a rewrite)

We don’t build these in v1, but the architecture must not preclude them:

  • The full world hierarchy already exists (Section 3) — Division/Conference/University/AthleticsDepartment/Team — so adding FCS→JUCO is new data, and the v2 economy (TV deals → budgets → NIL/recruiting/facilities) is new services on existing entities, not a restructure.
  • PlayCaller is an interface — a UserPlayCaller can replace the AI for the user’s team later (live play-calling) without touching PlayResolver.
  • The same play-by-play engine backs both watch and quick-sim (quick-sim just discards the play log), so spectator mode for non-player games is later a UI toggle to retain that log, not a new engine.
  • The event-driven core (Section 6) is open for new consumers — achievements, awards watches, social-media buzz, recruiting interest — which subscribe to existing events rather than touching the sim.
  • INarrativeGenerator lets the on-device LLM writer replace the templated baseline (Section 7) with no changes anywhere else.
  • The offseason stage is real in v1 (walk-on replenishment) — so recruiting, the transfer portal, and the coaching carousel plug into an existing seam, not a new one. A RecruitingService later replaces PlayerGenerator’s walk-on step behind the same call.
  • Player already carries class/year + career history, and the historical sidecar already archives it, so richer progression/aging is added depth, not new plumbing.
  • New per-module sidecars can be added without touching existing ones (Section 10) — by-ID references resolved through the registry mean a future heavy dataset (e.g. detailed player career logs) just becomes its own sidecar.
  • Everything resolves through CheckResolver, so balance changes are localized to modifier/DC curves.

13 Open Questions (to resolve before/while building)

  1. Exact attribute & skill list — needs its own attributes reference and the position→skill matrix + the attr → modifier and margin → yards curves.
  2. Play catalog — how granular is the playbook? (formations + concepts vs. abstract “run inside / play-action deep” buckets). Affects PlayCaller complexity.
  3. Clock & game-flow fidelity — real play-clock/game-clock model, quarter length scaling, hurry-up/2-minute logic.
  4. Penalties & special teams depth for v1.
  5. Data sourcing & generation — where real FBS team/conference identity data comes from, and the PlayerGenerator distributions (per-position, prestige-weighted) that yield believable initial rosters and walk-ons.
  6. CFP format specifics — pin the exact current bracket/seeding/bye rules and conference-champ auto-bids.
  7. Quick-sim performance — confirm full-engine quick-sim of a whole week (~65 games) is fast enough, or whether an abstracted fast path is needed.
  8. Local-LLM specifics — which ~7B model, quantization/size vs. quality, GGUF licensing for redistribution, and the per-week background-generation time budget.
  9. Narrative “script” design — the structured prompt/template per article type, and hallucination guardrails that constrain the model to NarrativeContext facts.
  10. Offseason depth & save growth — does v1’s offseason apply any development/aging or strictly advance years + walk-on replenish? And what’s the expected sidecar (esp. historical) growth over many seasons?

These are tracked as the next design passes; none block agreeing on the architecture above.
